CA Intoxicated Driving FAQ

Contact us for a consultation

Driving while intoxicated (DWI), operating a vehicle under the influence (OVI), or driving under the influence (DUI) are all ways to describe the same thing: intoxicated driving. Intoxication may be the result of alcohol or another chemical substance (for example marijuana) or both. To be a criminal offense in Lassen, Plumas, or Modoc counties, your blood alcohol content must be at or above the legal limit, you have an illegal chemical substance in your system, or your driving abilities were otherwise impaired.

In California, there are many arrests made daily for intoxicated driving. Those arrested are often law-abiding citizens.

What is blood alcohol content (BAC)?

Blood alcohol content (BAC) is a measurement of the amount of alcohol found in the blood expressed as a percentage. It is calculated in grams per 210 liters of breath, and a BAC of 0.08 means there is 0.08% alcohol by volume. Measuring BAC is a way for law enforcement to estimate alcohol consumption and impairment.

What are my rights during DUI traffic stops?

If you are pulled over due to suspicion of drunk driving, or pulled over for a traffic stop and an officer suspects intoxicated driving, remember you have certain rights.

  • You have the right to remain silent (you must still provide license, registration, and proof of insurance upon request).
  • If you are a passenger, you may be free to leave unless detained.
  • If you are arrested or detained, you have additional rights, including Miranda warnings.
  • You can state you wish to remain silent and ask for a lawyer.
  • You have the right to make a local phone call.

If you believe your rights were violated, write down everything you remember as soon as you can, including the agency, badge number, and patrol car number.

Can I refuse a breathalyzer test?

You can refuse a breathalyzer test. Portable breath tests are typically not admissible at trial in many situations. Refusal can still carry serious consequences depending on the facts and the jurisdiction.

  • Your license may be suspended.
  • You may face jail time or additional penalties.
  • You may still face a DUI charge based on other evidence (field sobriety tests, observations, witnesses).
  • You may be fined.

If you refuse, an officer may seek a warrant for a blood sample. Blood tests can be more reliable and may be more challenging to defend.

What are standardized field sobriety tests (FSTs)?

Standardized field sobriety tests (FSTs) are tests approved by the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) and are used to evaluate impairment.

There are three standardized FSTs:

  • Horizontal Gaze Nystagmus (HGN)
  • One-Leg Stand (OLS)
  • Walk-and-Turn

Non-standardized tests are generally less validated and may be challenged in court.

Examples of non-standardized FSTs include:

  • finger to nose
  • finger count
  • hand pat
  • alphabet
  • reverse counting
  • coin pickup

Can I refuse field sobriety tests in CA?

In many situations you may refuse field sobriety tests, though refusal can influence what the officer does next (including requesting other testing). Refusing field sobriety tests typically does not carry the same consequences as refusing chemical tests.

After a DUI arrest, will my license be suspended or revoked?

There are generally two processes: an administrative process (often DMV-related) and the criminal case. Administrative suspensions can happen even before a conviction depending on BAC results or refusal. A criminal conviction can also result in suspension or revocation depending on the facts, BAC level, and prior history.

What happens after a drunk driving arrest?

DUI arrests can involve both an administrative hearing and a criminal court process. Civil penalties may include license suspension; criminal penalties can include fines, jail, IID requirements, and other consequences.

Can I still get auto insurance after a DUI conviction?

Insurance availability depends on your record and whether it is a first conviction. Rates can increase significantly. You may be required to obtain SR-22 proof of financial responsibility in some situations. Fees vary.

Can I beat a drunk driving charge?

It may be possible to beat a charge, but it is rarely simple. These cases can involve technical issues with testing, procedure, and constitutional rights. Identifying errors and challenging unreliable evidence is often central to defense strategy.

Can I just plead guilty?

At arraignment, a defendant can plead guilty, no contest, or not guilty. Pleading guilty immediately can be a mistake without counsel.

  • You lose the opportunity to fight the charge.
  • You may lose leverage for negotiating a plea deal.
  • Sentencing can be harsher without time to mitigate and negotiate.
  • A conviction becomes your record and increases future exposure if there are later arrests.

Do I need a DUI lawyer?

If you plan to fight charges, representation can be critical. The law is complex and evidence can be technical. A defense attorney can evaluate stop legality, testing reliability, and constitutional issues and build a case plan.
